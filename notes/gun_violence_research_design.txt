Research Design

§1. Research question
Primary question: When race and structural inequity are decoupled (sampled across

=== PAGE 2 ===
cells where they vary independently rather than within-city where they covary), do
inequity features predict gun violence rates independently of race composition, or does
race composition retain independent predictive power beyond inequity features?
Secondary question: Which inequity-feature effects generalize across geographic
types (urban / suburban / rural) and which are specific to particular geographic
mechanisms?
Methodological question: Can the structural-prior partial-pooling framework that
identified BLK×Center coupling in Sloan and a_final residue classes in NBAProjections
identify analogous structural couplings in gun violence rate data across heterogeneous
geographic types?
§2. Cell definitions
Each cell is defined by demographics × inequity × geographic-type. Working-class
American profile match (§3) applied within each cell.
Geographic granularity per cell type
Urban cells: census tract (1,200–8,000 population). Population density
homogeneity at tract scale is appropriate for urban areas.
Suburban cells: census tract.
Rural cells: county. Rural counties are functionally one community at the scale
relevant to gun violence mechanisms.
Reservation cells: reservation as primary unit, with multiple-tribe aggregation if
needed.

§3. Working-Class American matching profile
Cells matched on the following dimensions to ensure cross-cell comparability on
baseline characteristics that aren’t the inequity features being tested.
Matching dimensions
(1) Household income. Median household income between 30th and 65th percentile of
national distribution. Excludes both extreme poverty (which has different dynamics) and
middle-class-and-above (which is outside the working-class definition).
(2) Education distribution. Working-class education profile: ≥40% high school
graduate, ≤30% bachelor’s degree, balance of some-college / associate’s. Filters out
both highly-educated affluent areas and areas with extremely low educational
attainment that may have different dynamics.
(3) Labor force participation. Employed civilian labor force ≥55% of working-age
population. Filters out areas with extreme unemployment or out-of-labor-force
populations.
(4) Household structure. Married-couple-with-children + single-parent +
multigenerational households combined ≥50%. Filters out extreme outliers (e.g.,
college-town tracts with mostly student households).
(5) Age structure. Working-age population (18-64) ≥55%. Filters out extreme outliers
(retirement communities, military bases).
(6) Stable population. Population change between recent ACS 5-year estimates ≤25%
absolute. Filters out areas in rapid demographic transition that may have unstable
outcome measurement.
Matching algorithm
Coarsened Exact Matching (CEM). Each dimension discretized into 3-5 bins, exact
match required across all bins.
Rationale: CEM is interpretable, robust, doesn’t require parametric assumptions about
propensity score functional form. Standard in observational epidemiology for cross-

=== PAGE 5 ===
cohort matching.
Alternative considered: Entropy Balancing. Rejected because it makes the matching less
transparent and the structural-prior framework already handles continuous covariate
adjustment in the model itself.

§4. Outcome variable
Primary outcome
Gun violence rate per 100,000 population per year, averaged over a 5-year window
(2019-2023 or most recent 5 years with complete data).
Includes:
Homicide by firearm (CDC WISQARS underlying cause of death, ICD-10 X93-X95,
U01.4)
Suicide by firearm (CDC WISQARS, ICD-10 X72-X74, Y22-Y24)
Non-fatal injury by firearm (GVA incident records geocoded to unit)
Excluded:
Police-involved shootings (different mechanism, separate cell typology needed)
Accidental shootings (different mechanism, sparse counts)
Mass shootings as a category (rare events, statistically unstable at cell level)
Secondary outcomes
For sensitivity analyses:
Homicide rate only (without suicide)
Suicide rate only (different mechanism, included as separate analysis)
Non-fatal injury rate only (GVA dependent)

Cells differ in reporting infrastructure quality. Adjustment factor per cell type:
Urban with good local PD reporting: 1.0 (reference)

framework. The model treats reporting rate as a latent variable per cell type with
informed prior. We don’t pretend the adjustment is exact.

§6. Pre-registered hypotheses
Each hypothesis specifies what the cross-cell pattern would look like under different
causal structures.
H_INEQUITY (the existing-literature consensus position)
Statement: Inequity features predict gun violence rates. Race composition predicts
only through correlation with inequity features. After conditioning on the inequity
feature set, race adds no significant independent predictive power.
Predicted pattern in decoupled cells:
SB-MC (Black-majority, low inequity): low gun violence rates, comparable to UW-LI
RW-HI (white-majority, high inequity): high gun violence rates, comparable to other
high-inequity cells
Cross-cell variation in rates tracks inequity composite, not race composition
Race coefficient in joint model approximately zero after inequity adjustment
Falsification criterion: Race composition contributes ≥X% additional variance beyond
inequity features in joint model. Specific X to be set after power analysis but before
outcome data inspection — current target X = 15%.
H_RACE_PURE
Statement: Race composition independently predicts gun violence rates beyond what
inequity features explain. Some causal pathway operates through race-specific
mechanisms not captured by measured inequity features.
Predicted pattern in decoupled cells:
SB-MC: gun violence rates elevated relative to UW-LI despite comparable inequity
RW-HI: gun violence rates not as elevated as urban Black-majority cells despite
comparable inequity
Race coefficient retains significance after inequity adjustment
Falsification criterion: Race composition contributes <5% additional variance beyond
inequity features. The hypothesis would be substantially weakened.
H_INTERACTION
Statement: Race × inequity interaction effects predict gun violence rates. Neither

=== PAGE 10 ===
marginal effect is sufficient; the joint co-occurrence is what matters.
Predicted pattern:
Maximum gun violence rates in cells where high-inequity Black-majority intersect
(UB-HI > sum of marginal effects)
Off-diagonal cells (SB-MC, RW-HI) have intermediate rates
Interaction term is significant in joint model
Falsification criterion: Race × inequity interaction explains <3% additional variance
beyond main effects.
H_HISTORICAL_MECHANISM
Statement: Specific historical mechanisms (redlining, Reconstruction violence,
economic collapse, treaty violations) explain geographic variation in gun violence rates
beyond contemporary inequity features. The mechanism, not the current condition, is
the causal driver.
Predicted pattern:
Cells with same contemporary inequity profile but different historical mechanisms
have different gun violence rates
Urban Black-majority high-inequity (post-redlining mechanism) ≠ Rural Black-
majority high-inequity (post-Reconstruction mechanism) ≠ Rural white-majority
high-inequity (post-industrial collapse mechanism)
Historical mechanism markers add significant predictive power in joint model
Falsification criterion: Historical mechanism markers add <5% additional variance
beyond contemporary inequity features.
H_GEOGRAPHIC_MECHANISM
Statement: Specific inequity features have different effects in different geographic
types. The food-access effect in urban tracts works through different intermediate
pathways than the food-access effect in rural counties.
Predicted pattern:
Same inequity feature has different effect sizes (and possibly different signs) across
geographic types
Geographic-type × inequity-feature interaction is significant

=== PAGE 11 ===
Findings stratified by geographic type rather than averaged
Falsification criterion: Geographic-type × inequity-feature interactions consistently
<2% additional variance beyond main effects.
Outcome possibilities
The hypotheses are not mutually exclusive. The actual finding could be:
H_INEQUITY + H_GEOGRAPHIC_MECHANISM (inequity drives, but through different
pathways by geography)
H_HISTORICAL_MECHANISM + H_INTERACTION (historical mechanism produces
specific contemporary inequity × race interactions)
H_RACE_PURE + H_HISTORICAL_MECHANISM (residual race effect after inequity
adjustment is explained by historical mechanism)
Various other combinations
The pre-registration is what each hypothesis WOULD predict, not which hypothesis we
expect to find.
§7 . Power-stratified claim framework
Each cell has different statistical power based on post-matching sample size. Claims
about each cell are pre-stratified by the power tier.
Tier 1: Strong claims (well-powered cells)
Cells: UB-HI, UH-HI, UW-LI, SB-MC, RW-HI
Sample sizes: post-matching ≥120 units per cell
Claim language permitted: “Effect estimated at X with 95% CI [Y, Z]. Effect
generalizes within this cell type. ”
Effect sizes detectable: ≥10% relative variance contribution with 80% power
Tier 2: Moderate claims (adequately-powered cells)
Cells: RB-HI, RH-HI
Sample sizes: post-matching 25-100 units per cell
Claim language permitted: “Effect consistent with [direction]. Estimated effect size X

=== PAGE 12 ===
but cell-specific power limits precision; interpret with caveats. Findings hypothesized
but not independently confirmed at cell level. ”
Effect sizes detectable: ≥20% relative variance contribution with 80% power
Tier 3: Hypothesis-generating (under-powered cells)
Cells: RNA-HI
Sample sizes: post-matching 8-18 units
Claim language permitted: “Observed pattern consistent with [direction]. Cell-
specific findings hypothesis-generating only. Larger study needed for independent
confirmation. Pattern contributes to overall structural prior but not separately tested. ”
Effect sizes detectable: ≥40% relative variance contribution with 80% power, or
strong qualitative patterns
Cross-cell claims
Claims about cross-cell patterns (the decoupling hypothesis itself) use the joint sample
across all cells. The full sample is well-powered for cross-cell effects even when
individual cells are not. Cross-cell claim power is calculated separately.

Step 3: Hierarchical Bayesian model
Structural-prior partial-pooling framework. Model specification:
Priors:
Cell-type effects: hierarchical normal with shrinkage to overall mean
Race effects: hierarchical normal
Inequity effects: weakly informative normal centered on direction from existing

Interactions: tighter shrinkage (regularizing prior to avoid overfitting)
Reporting-rate adjustment: informed by validation studies
Inference via Stan (HMC) or PyMC. Multiple chains, convergence diagnostics, posterior
predictive checks.

§9. Threats to validity and mitigations
Threat 1: Reporting-rate adjustment mis-specification
If the reporting-rate adjustment factors are systematically wrong, cells with under-

=== PAGE 15 ===
counted incidents will appear to have lower true rates than they do.
Mitigation: (a) Propagate adjustment uncertainty through the model. (b) Run sensitivity
analysis with alternative adjustment specifications. (c) Cross-validate with WISQARS
mortality (which has uniform coverage) where available.
Threat 2: Working-class profile mis-specification
If the matching profile inadvertently excludes important variation, findings restrict to a
more specific population than intended.
Mitigation: (a) Pre-register the profile definition explicitly. (b) Report sample
composition before analysis. (c) Sensitivity analysis with alternative profile cutoffs.
Threat 3: Geographic granularity mismatch
Urban tracts and rural counties differ in size by orders of magnitude. Aggregating at
different scales can introduce artifacts.
Mitigation: (a) Explicit geographic-type indicator in the model. (b) Multi-scale partial
pooling. (c) Report effects per-100,000 standardized.
Threat 4: Inequity feature collinearity
The inequity features are correlated with each other and with race composition.
Identifying independent effects is challenging.
Mitigation: (a) Report joint model and marginal models. (b) Use cells where features
decouple (the SB-MC and RW-HI cells exist for this). (c) Sensitivity analysis with feature
subsets.
Threat 5: Causal interpretation
The design is observational. Findings are associational. Causal claims require additional
assumptions.
Mitigation: (a) Frame findings as predictive associations, not causal effects. (b)
Discuss causal interpretation explicitly in limitations. (c) Note that decoupled-cell
design provides stronger evidence for some causal pathways than within-city literature
but doesn’t establish causality.
Threat 6: Political reception
Findings will be politicized regardless of methodology.
Mitigation: (a) Pre-registration with public timestamp before analysis. (b) Report

findings whatever they show. (c) Methodologically rigorous framing makes selective use
harder. (d) Cross-cutting findings (some support each side) reduce one-sided framing
power.
